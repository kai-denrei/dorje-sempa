The spine of the whole subject

Three things, often confused

Almost every misunderstanding of this topic comes from collapsing three separate things into one. Pulling them apart is the first and most useful move.

ཀརྨ་བཀའ་བརྒྱུད་ — Karma Kagyu
A roughly 900-year-old Tibetan Buddhist lineage — the largest sub-school of the broader བཀའ་བརྒྱུད་ tradition, headed by the Karmapa. It is monastic and yogic: real monasteries, abbots, a scholastic curriculum, and three-year retreats, now found across Tibet, India, Nepal, Bhutan and worldwide.
Diamond Way Buddhism
Something far more specific: a worldwide network of lay (non-monastic) centers founded in the early 1970s by the Danish teacher Ole Nydahl (“Lama Ole,” 1941–2026) and his wife Hannah Nydahl (1946–2007), at the request of the 16th Karmapa. By Ole Nydahl's death it numbered on the order of 600–650 centers in more than 40 countries.
Vajrayāna
The tantric “diamond vehicle” of Buddhism as a whole (Sanskrit vajra = diamond / thunderbolt, yāna = vehicle). “Diamond Way” is simply the plain-English rendering of this word — which is the naming trap: lowercase, it means all of Vajrayāna; capitalized, it means Nydahl's organization.

In one sentence: Diamond Way is one modern, Western, lay organization operating within the older Karma Kagyu lineage — and it sides with one party in a contested succession dispute inside that lineage.

The golden rosary

The Lineage

Tibetan Buddhism is conventionally divided into four schools: the “old” Nyingma and the three “new” schools — Kagyu, Sakya and Gelug. བཀའ་བརྒྱུད་ (Kagyu) is usually glossed as the lineage of the Buddha's word, or more evocatively the oral, whispered transmission — a tradition that prizes the direct passing of meditative realization from teacher to student over textual scholasticism.

Its founding line, the གསེར་ཕྲེང་ or “golden rosary,” runs from a primordial buddha through a chain of Indian adepts and Tibetan masters into the line of the Karmapas. Each is a bead on the rosary; the trunk is unbroken.

The Black Crown Lama

The Karmapa & the Black Crown

The Karmapa (ཀརྨ་པ་) is popularly the Black Crown Lama. Tradition holds that he wears an immaterial “wisdom crown” woven from the hair of ḍākinīs, perceptible only to the realized. A physical Vajra (Black) Crown — a material replica — was, by tradition, gifted by the Ming Yongle Emperor to the 5th Karmapa in the early 1400s. The ceremonial display of it, the Black Crown Ceremony, was held to confer liberation on those who witnessed it, and became a signature public rite of the 16th Karmapa's tours of the West.

The Karmapa line carries a deeper institutional first. It is generally regarded as the earliest formally recognized reincarnate-teacher (སྤྲུལ་སྐུ་) lineage — the model later used for the Dalai Lamas and thousands of other tulkus. The 2nd Karmapa, Karma Pakshi (1204–1283), was the first recognized this way, and was a noted figure at the Mongol court of Möngke and Kublai Khan.

Around the Karmapa stand a set of high reincarnate lines that recognize one another and administer the lineage during a Karmapa's minority — the “four regents.” The most important are the ཞྭ་དམར་པ་ (Shamarpa), the “Red Crown” lama historically regarded as a near-counterpart to the Karmapa (the 14th, Mipham Chökyi Lodrö, 1952–2014, is central to the modern dispute); Tai Situ Rinpoche (the 12th, Pema Donyö Nyinje, b. 1954); Gyaltsab Rinpoche; and Jamgön Kongtrul Rinpoche. The honorific རིན་པོ་ཆེ་, “precious one,” marks these recognized masters.

The lineage's seats trace a history of exile: from Tsurphu, the historic main seat in central Tibet, to Rumtek in Sikkim, India, re-established after 1959 — and itself now a focus of the succession litigation.

Two recognitions

The Karmapa Controversy

You cannot place Diamond Way in the Buddhist world without this dispute, because it sits firmly on one side of it. When the 16th Karmapa died in 1981 with no universally accepted recognition of his successor, the process of finding the reincarnation fractured. By the early 1990s the result was two separately enthroned claimants to the title of 17th Karmapa.

This site takes no position on who is “the” Karmapa. Both are presented in parallel, with recognitions attributed to whoever made them.

Ogyen Trinley Dorje b. 1985

  • Recognized by Tai Situ Rinpoche & Gyaltsab Rinpoche, on the basis of a prediction letter attributed to the 16th Karmapa.
  • Confirmed by the 14th Dalai Lama; accepted by the Chinese government.
  • Enthroned at Tsurphu in 1992; fled Tibet for India in 2000.
  • Holds the recognition of the large majority of Karma Kagyu monasteries and lamas. (Critics' label: “Beijing Karmapa.”)
  • Official site: kagyuoffice.org

Trinley Thaye Dorje b. 1983

  • Recognized by the 14th Shamarpa, who disputed the prediction letter's authenticity.
  • Brought out of Tibet to India in 1994.
  • Stepped back from the monastic role to marry in 2017 (wife Rinchen Yangzom; son Thugsey); teaches as a lay-friendly Karmapa.
  • Backed by an influential minority — most prominently Diamond Way.
  • Official site: karmapa.org

When Diamond Way materials say “the 17th Karmapa,” they mean Thaye Dorje. Most other Karma Kagyu institutions mean Ogyen Trinley Dorje. Neither side disputes that the lineage and its teachings are shared; the dispute is over the person.

A Western lay transmission

Diamond Way

In 1968 Ole and Hannah Nydahl, a young Danish couple, traveled to Nepal and met Tibetan teachers; in 1969 they became, by their own account, among the first Western students of the 16th Karmapa. After a period of study and practice — including the meditation of conscious dying, འཕོ་བ་ — the Karmapa asked them to return to Europe and begin founding lay centers, establishing Karma Kagyu practice for Western householders.

From the first center in the early 1970s the network grew steadily across five decades. Ole Nydahl became a near-constant traveler and was eventually recognized as a བླ་མ་ — one of very few Westerners to hold that role within the tradition. Hannah Nydahl worked extensively as a translator for high Tibetan lamas and was a quieter but essential architect of the movement until her death from cancer in 2007; her life is the subject of the documentary Hannah: Buddhism's Untold Story.

The organization's distinctive profile: lay, not monastic — members have jobs, families and ordinary lives; volunteer-run and decentralized, with no paid clergy; bound for its whole history by a single principal teacher and the travelling teachers he authorized; and marked by an emphasis on accessibility, directness and a confident, this-worldly tone.

In 2026 the founder's era closed. Ole Nydahl turned 85 on 19 March and died on 18 May 2026 at the Europe Center in Immenstadt, in the Bavarian Alps, with family present and students having gathered to meditate in the preceding weeks. After the traditional post-death meditative process (ཐུགས་དམ་), Thaye Dorje — whom Diamond Way recognizes as the 17th Karmapa — opened the farewell ceremonies on 21 May 2026. A network of some 600 centers built around one founder now faces the classic question of continuity after a charismatic founder. As of late May 2026 this is live, unfolding history.

Balanced, sourced

Teaching Style & Critical Perspectives

Ole Nydahl was, and remains after his death in May 2026, a genuinely contested figure — admired and criticized in roughly equal measure, often for the very same things. What follows gives fair weight to each thread, and resists both hagiography and takedown.

Style and biography

An ex-boxer from Copenhagen, Nydahl was candid in his autobiography that before his conversion he and Hannah funded their travels partly by smuggling hashish, and that he served prison time — he framed his life as proof that a “tough guy” could transform. Onstage he was high-energy, blunt, funny and deliberately provocative, talking frankly about sex and death; he remained an avid motorcyclist and skydiver into old age, surviving a near-fatal parachute accident in 2003. Followers found this liberating — a Buddhism for confident, worldly people. Critics found it shallow, with some attendees describing his Mahāmudrā courses as rambling between recited verses.

The teaching on sexuality

Because Diamond Way practitioners are lay yogis, disturbing emotions — desire included — are treated as raw material for enlightenment rather than something to suppress. Nydahl framed the Buddha's teaching on sexuality in three levels: monastic celibacy, for those who struggle with relationships; ordinary lay partnership, built on mutual joy and the classic tantric “diamond” and “lotus” imagery; and an advanced “accomplisher” level of consciously steering subtle energies, which he said required mastery of practices such as inner heat first.

The criticisms

The serious objection is this: Nydahl acknowledged sexual relationships with female students, arguing that no teacher–student relationship was involved in them — which critics rejected, because the lama / devotee power asymmetry does not simply switch off. Former members described the culture as elitist and sexist. The scholar Burkhard (Bee) Scherer analyzes a pervasive “hetero-machismo,” pre-modern gender stereotyping and a mildly homophobic tone; one influential 2011 study is titled “Macho Buddhism.” A broader critique bundles a hedonism-and-extreme-sports image (dubbed “lifestyle Buddhism”), militaristic aesthetics, and anti-Islam political rhetoric. The mixing of sex and teaching sparked a semi-public 2010 disagreement with the 14th Shamarpa.

A note on positionality: Scherer is himself a Karma Kagyu practitioner under Thaye Dorje, with former Diamond Way ties — a vantage point worth keeping in view when weighing the analysis.

The scholarly middle ground

It is important to resist equating this with the documented abuse scandals at Rigpa (Sogyal Rinpoche) or Shambhala. Scherer explicitly argues that the reality is more complex and less sensationalist: the relationships were, on the public record, between adults, and were not subject to the coercion-and-cover-up allegations that brought down those groups. The substantive objection is structural — about power and consent — together with gender politics, not documented predation.

A fair verdict: a teacher whose deliberate breaking of the “holy guru” template drove both his enormous appeal and the steady criticism — and on which informed people, including scholars sympathetic to his lineage, genuinely land differently.

Foundation → guru yoga → deity → Mahāmudrā

Practices

Diamond Way teaches a path that is recognizably Karma Kagyu in content, organized for lay practitioners. Its architecture is the classic Vajrayāna sequence — foundation → guru yoga → deity practice → Mahāmudrā — with Phowa as a signature.

At the level of method, Diamond Way describes meditation as two movements: first calming the mind (Tibetan shinay), then the insight (Tibetan lhaktong) that recognizes the nature of mind itself.

སྔོན་འགྲོ་ Ngöndro — the foundations

“That which goes before”: four foundational practices, each traditionally accumulated 111,111 times (the extra digits “for mistakes”). Together they purify obscurations and gather the two accumulations of merit and wisdom.

  1. 1

    Refuge & prostrations

    Going for refuge (སྐྱབས་འགྲོ་) to the Three Jewels and the lineage, with full-body prostrations; purifies pride.

  2. 2

    Vajrasattva (Dorje Sempa)

    Purification through the buddha Vajrasattva and his 100-syllable mantra — cleansing harmful impressions.

  3. 3

    Mandala offerings

    Symbolically offering the entire universe; cultivates generosity and gathers merit.

  4. 4

    Guru yoga

    Uniting one's mind with the enlightened mind of the lama and lineage — in Diamond Way, the 16th Karmapa meditation.

The 16th Karmapa Meditation — the daily guru yoga

The defining everyday practice of Diamond Way: visualize the 16th Karmapa in his Black Crown form, receive the lights of his body, speech and mind, dissolve the form into oneself, and rest in the resulting state. It binds a global lay དགེ་འདུན་ (sangha) together and serves as a condensed gateway to Mahāmudrā.

Featured practice

Phowa — the meditation of conscious dying

འཕོ་བ་

Phowa · 'pho ba · “transference”

འཕོ་བ་ is the practice of consciously ejecting consciousness at the moment of death through the crown of the head into a pure realm — the buddha Amitābha's pure land of Dewachen (Sukhāvatī). It is one of the Six Yogas of Nāropa and, in the Karma Kagyu, can be transmitted relatively accessibly.

Ole Nydahl began teaching Phowa in 1987 at the request of Shamar Rinpoche and transmitted it to tens of thousands of people in intensive courses; mastery of Phowa was a major part of his public identity and one of Diamond Way's signature offerings.

ཡི་དམ་ Yidam — deity practice

After Ngöndro, with དབང་ (empowerment), a practitioner may take up a yidam: a meditational buddha-form embodying an enlightened quality — སྤྱན་རས་གཟིགས་ (Chenrezig / Avalokiteśvara) for compassion, Tara for active protection, Vajrasattva for purification. The method is visualization, mantra, and identification with the deity as a reflection of one's own enlightened nature.

ཕྱག་ཆེན་ Mahāmudrā — the Great Seal

The culminating view and meditation of the Kagyu lineage — its crown jewel. A direct recognition of the nature of mind as the inseparable union of emptiness and luminous awareness, beyond conceptual elaboration. It is approached by sūtra (analytical), tantra (energy / yidam-based), or essence (direct introduction by a qualified lama) routes.

The Six Yogas of Nāropa

The advanced yogic curriculum of the Kagyu — typically undertaken in three-year retreat, part of the lineage's inheritance more than everyday lay content. Phowa is the sixth. Here a yoga (Tibetan naljor, “union”) is a discipline of practice that joins one with enlightened reality, not a set of physical postures.

  • གཏུམ་མོ་

    Tummo

    Inner heat — manipulating the subtle body's channels, winds and drops.

  • སྒྱུ་ལུས་

    Illusory body (Gyulü)

    Recognizing the dream-like, illusory nature of body and phenomena.

  • རྨི་ལམ་

    Dream yoga (Milam)

    Achieving lucidity and practicing within the dream state.

  • འོད་གསལ་

    Clear light (Ösel)

    Recognizing the luminous nature of mind, especially in deep sleep and at death.

  • བར་དོ་

    Bardo yoga

    Practice for the intermediate state between death and rebirth.

  • འཕོ་བ་

    Phowa

    Transference of consciousness at death — the signature practice above.
